The Architecture of Network Planning

 

 

Many know the humble beginnings of the Southwest Airlines network, a triangle scrawled onto a cocktail napkin one fateful San Antonio night. But fewer know the story of our network today.

It might not be as easily drawn as the original, but the network which Southwest Airlines currently flies is still the result of the ingenuity and brilliance that our People bring to the table. However, as the network has grown, so has the need for Technology to support the Network Planning Team and their efforts.

Southwest flies to over a hundred destinations, most of which are serviced many times a day. To operate such a large network, the Network Planning team has to answer some tough questions: Where’s the most demand? How do we accommodate that demand with the aircraft we have? Should we service a new destination?



The Network Planning Tools


In order to create and maintain optimized networks and schedules, Network Planning utilizes highly-complex mathematical models and network planning applications. These tools help them understand how changes to our network could affect our revenue streams, operational performance, and other key metrics. Although no tool can replace the expertise and judgment of a skilled Network Planning Team, these tools helps get the most out of human talent.

Some of the tools Network planning uses include a tool to simulate consumer demand, another for large schedule and route changes, and even one designed to make smaller changes in the network for nimble responses to weather, taking aircraft out of service, or other events which require immediate attention. These tools are all housed within the larger SkySuite Network Planning solution.



Network Planning and Architecture


A key architectural challenge associated with Network Planning technology is contained within the sheer volume of calculations and computations the network planning applications must perform. Southwest has a unique challenge in this space with respect to other large airlines: our point-to-point network.

Traditional airlines like United or Delta use what are called “hub-and-spoke” networks. Large airports like DFW International or Hartsfield Atlanta International act as central locations (hubs) through which routes are flown (spokes).

With most flights being routed through just a handful of large airports, fewer flight legs overall are required to fly from any given airport within the network to another.

 

An example of a Hub-and-Spoke network

 

This stands in contrast with the point-to-point network that Southwest Airlines uses. Instead of routes being flown through hubs, each airport within the network can be connected to as many or as few airports as we’d like. Each route exists independently of a hub, because there are none.

 

An example of a Point-to-Point network

 


The flexibility of the point-to-point network is a key strategic asset for our company, as well as other low-cost carriers. However, that flexibility is what creates the architectural challenges associated with the supporting technology; the number of possible ways to configure a point-to-point network are far greater than a hub-and-spoke network. More possibilities means more computation, a lot more.

To handle the intense computation executed by our Network Planning solutions, we’ve placed them in a Cloud environment. This gives the solution access to as much computational power as it needs, but only when it needs it. Computational resources aren’t sitting idle when the tools aren’t being used, and greater computational power is available as needed.



How an Architectural Approach Fits In


Getting the right tools for the challenge, and implementing those tools in the right environments is what an architectural approach is all about. Each solution is different, and should be tailored to the challenge at hand.

We in Architecture are proud of the collaborative efforts and solutions we create for our CoHearts in other areas of the company. And today, we say thank you to Network Planning.

 

 

The Platform Council

 

 

As Southwest continues to build our Cloud and DevOps foundation, a need was identified to accelerate decisions related to platforms and services. In order to meet that need, the Platform Council and supporting teams were formed.

You may have already read or heard about the Platform Council from Managing Director Lauren Woods, or Chief Technology Officer Tom Merritt at an all-hands meeting, or in another email. Architecture would like to take a moment to explain how the Platform Council works once more, for anyone who hasn’t heard or wants to hear it again.


The Goals


The goals of the Platform Council are as follows:

  • Accelerate Enterprise Platform Decisions
  • Ensure Standardization of Platform Decisions
  • Improve Communications of Platform Decisions

 
While Architecture remains responsible for many strategic decisions of how systems are configured to work with one another, the Platform Council drives nimble decision making associated with Cloud and DevOps services and platforms which adhere to those strategic decisions.
 

 

 

Participants in the Platform Council bridge across multiple groups, including Architecture, and many others. While the Platform Council is accelerating decision making associated with Cloud and DevOps services and platforms, all relevant stakeholders are involved. Completeness and alignment are not compromised for speed.
